Bedouins are nomads who live in the deserts of the Middle East and North Africa. In the past, they were known as the wanderers of the desert because they travelled by camel, moving from oasis to oasis with their herds of sheep, goats and camels. They had to constantly move because of the hard desert environment in which they lived. They travelled in small groups of related tribes. The leaders of those groups were called sheikhs, and this position was handed down from father to son for generations.

Bedouins are famous for their hospitality and generosity. This comes from living in one of the hottest and driest regions on Earth, which gives them a shared respect for the dangers of the desert. Fellow travellers are invited into their tents and offered spiced coffee. As well as being courageous, Bedouins are also independent and resourceful people, able to survive in some of the harshest deserts through their knowledge of the seasons and the location of water.

Today's Bedouins are not as traditional as their ancestors; they are more likely to settle on the outskirts of villages or towns and travel by car. This is because their countries have developed and new towns and villages have been built. As a result, Bedouins have less land available for their herds than they used to. Despite this, some Bedouins still live in the desert, maintaining their way of life, honouring the simple grace and beauty of nature.
